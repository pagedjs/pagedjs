A brief essay

Moveable type did not arrive in Europe as a single invention but as a confluence of crafts. The goldsmith’s punch, the bookbinder’s press, the papermaker’s mould, and the scrivener’s ink each had long histories of their own; what changed in the middle of the fifteenth century, in a workshop near Mainz, was the assembly of these familiar tools into a single reproducible system. Johannes Gutenberg cast individual letter-sorts in a type-metal alloy soft enough to strike cleanly yet hard enough to survive the press, and he arranged them by hand into the neat columns that the reader would later mistake for the natural form of a page.

The first products of his press were not books at all. Indulgences, printed one side at a time from a locked forme of type, paid the bills while the great Bible took shape at the back of the shop. That Bible, finished around 1455, imitated the dense black-letter script of contemporary manuscripts so exactly that early buyers are said to have refused to believe it was not hand-written. Its forty-two lines per column, two columns per page, set the visual grammar that would guide European typography for a generation.

The craft travelled swiftly. Within twenty-five years, presses were at work in Cologne, Strasbourg, Augsburg, Venice, Paris, Rome, and London. Each city contributed something of its own: the Venetians, under the direction of Aldus Manutius, refined the roman and italic letterforms that still furnish the bulk of our reading matter; the Parisians gave the trade its first great dynasty of type founders in the Estiennes; the Nurembergers sent woodcuts and mathematical diagrams through the same press runs as their prayer books. A technology invented to reproduce the Latin Bible had, within a lifetime, begun to reshape every branch of learning it touched.

A Timeline of Early European Printing

Year Place Event
c. 1439 Strasbourg Gutenberg experiments with metal type during his exile from Mainz.
c. 1450 Mainz Gutenberg, Fust, and Schöffer establish a printing workshop financed by Johann Fust’s loans.
c. 1454 Mainz Printing of the 31-line Indulgence, the earliest dated European printed work.
c. 1455 Mainz Completion of the 42-line Bible, the first major book printed with moveable type.
1457 Mainz Fust and Schöffer publish the Mainz Psalter, the first book to carry a printed colophon and date.
1465 Subiaco Sweynheym and Pannartz open the first press in Italy, at the Benedictine monastery of Subiaco.
1470 Paris The first Parisian press is established at the Sorbonne by Gering, Crantz, and Friburger.
1476 Westminster William Caxton sets up the first press in England, having learned the trade in Bruges.
1495 Venice Aldus Manutius issues De Aetna, cut in the first roman type by Francesco Griffo.
1501 Venice Aldus introduces italic type and the octavo format with his portable editions of the Latin classics.
1516 Basel Froben publishes Erasmus’ Greek New Testament, setting new standards for scholarly typography.
1539 Mexico City Juan Pablos opens the first press in the Americas.

Afterword

A table of dates flattens a story that was in truth a web of connections: apprentices who travelled from city to city, patrons who underwrote new workshops, legal quarrels that sent technical secrets out of one shop and into another. The press, once established in a city, rarely stood alone; it drew to itself a quarter of binders, illustrators, paper merchants, and booksellers, and in doing so shaped the urban geography of the book trade for centuries. What the timeline preserves is the shape of a wave; the essay’s task is to remind us that every wave is made of water.
